The Business Model: Website Audit as a Service (WaaS)

A repeatable approach for freelance web developers and founders: find small businesses with broken or underperforming websites, give them a free diagnosis, then sell the fix.

The Core Idea

You find small businesses with bad websites, show them exactly what's wrong for free, then charge them to fix it. The audit is the sales tool. The fix is the product.

Why It Works

Most small hospitality businesses have a website because someone told them they needed one — but nobody ever told them whether it was actually working. When you show up and say "here's exactly what's costing you bookings," you're solving a problem they already have but didn't know they could fix.

How It Works — Step by Step

  • Identify pubs, cafes, tradesmen, and other small businesses with a website that's likely losing customers.
  • Review poor UX, slow load times, bad mobile design, or missing calls to action.
  • Record a short 2–3 minute video audit showing the problems in plain language.
  • Send it unsolicited for free and wait for replies from interested business owners.
  • Offer to fix the issues and convert the free audit into paid work.

The Revenue Ladder

  • Start with a low-cost audit report for €25–50 to open the door.
  • Upgrade to a €100–200 improvement roadmap after the first review.
  • Deliver actual Wix Studio implementation at €200–500+ once trust is built.
  • Scale into monthly retainers for ongoing updates and maintenance.

What Makes It Zero-Budget

No paid tools needed. Loom, email, Google Maps and AI prompting are all free or low-cost. Your time and web development skill are the only real investment.

The Scalability Path

After 5–10 case studies, charge upfront for audits. After 10–15 retained clients, hire a junior developer to handle implementation while you focus on sales and audits. The audit becomes a productised process.

In One Sentence

You trade a free 3-minute video for the right to have a sales conversation, and you win that conversation by already having solved half the problem before they've said yes.
